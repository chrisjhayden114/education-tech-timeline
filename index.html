How the Fear Dial is scored

Every technology carries a learning/cognitive panic dial from 0 to 5. These scores are not calculated by a formula — they are retrospective judgment calls about the size of the historical panic, not whether the fear proved true.

What the dial measures

Peak education-related fear intensity at the historical high point of the panic: roughly intensity × reach. A quiet scholarly worry scores low; a mass panic that pulls in schools, courts, parliament, or health authorities scores high. The dial measures the size of the fear, not its accuracy.

How a score is assigned

  1. Contemporaneous claims — What did people at the time say the technology would do to memory, attention, literacy, reasoning, or skill formation?
  2. Scale and persistence — Was the worry niche or public? Brief or sustained? Did it produce bans, burnings, curriculum fights, or lasting policy settlements?
  3. Learning focus — How directly did the panic target schooling or cognitive development (versus morality, health, or public order alone)?
  4. Reach at peak — How widely did the fear circulate among parents, teachers, journalists, and institutions? Tiny elite debates stay low even when intense; mass panics climb the dial.

The scale

  • 0 None — no meaningful learning/cognitive panic
  • 2 Low — documented worry, limited reach or short-lived
  • 3 Moderate — clear public learning panic
  • 4 High — major, multi-institution panic
  • 5 Severe — mass panic with lasting settlement or prohibition

Related codings (fear type Yes/Partial/No, engines, and control) use the same judgment-based method. Some added entries (*) are scored retrospectively from the historical record. A short reference version also appears in the Legend & Notes.

The dial & the engines

The dial (intensity)

A scale of 0–5 measuring the intensity of the peak panic in public discourse. A dial of 5 means a mass panic involving multiple institutions and a lasting settlement or prohibition.

0 1 2 3 4 5

The engines (mechanism)

Cognitive panics are driven by four engines of anxiety. Most fire one or two; a panic that fires all four is rare. Assessment centrality of the substituted skill helps explain how high the dial climbs when substitution is involved.

  • Substitution Weakening the mind by doing for it what it should do itself — the skill never forms.
  • Displacement Taking time away from better activities — study time crowded out.
  • Integrity Whose work is it? Enabling cheating or deception.
  • Contamination Exposing the mind to content that warps it.

The Pattern

Across all 67 technologies, one variable does most of the work: who controls the moment of use. Every panic that reached public scale attached to a technology children used autonomously; the technologies that quietly transformed schooling were controlled by teachers and institutions from birth. Transformation and panic turn out to be nearly orthogonal.
